A property lead replies on WhatsApp at 11:40 AM — "Is the 3BHK still available? Can I see it this weekend?" Your rep doesn't see it until 4 PM, because they were working the enquiry list top to bottom and that lead came in at position 38. By then the buyer has booked a site visit with the project down the road. A ₹1.4 Cr deal, gone, not because the rep was lazy — because the list was sorted by arrival time instead of by intent.

A **real estate priority queue** fixes exactly this. Instead of a chronological enquiry list, you maintain a live, ranked order of every lead — sorted by how likely each one is to book a site visit right now. The hottest lead is always at the top. The rep works down from there. This post is the step-by-step way to build one.

## TL;DR

- An enquiry list sorted by arrival time buries your best leads. A real estate priority queue ranks them by likelihood to book a site visit instead.
- Five inputs carry the score: budget fit (against your GCV band), location serviceability, configuration match, portal behaviour, and WhatsApp reply speed.
- The queue must re-rank in real time — a WhatsApp "yes" should move a lead from Grade C to Grade A inside the same shift.
- Reps work it top-down: first three calls of the day go to the three leads most likely to convert, not the three oldest.
- Grade A property leads convert when contacted within roughly 47 minutes. A queue exists to protect that window.

## Why arrival order quietly loses deals

Most real estate desks run on a spreadsheet or a CRM list sorted newest-first. It feels fair — first in, first called. The trouble is it treats a serious ₹2 Cr buyer who downloaded the brochure twice the same as a tyre-kicker who filled a Housing.com form to see a floor plan and vanished.

In a typical project, leads arrive across 99acres, MagicBricks, Housing, the project microsite, and walk-in WhatsApp numbers. On a busy launch weekend you might log 60–90 enquiries a day. A rep can seriously work maybe 25–30 of them. So the question that decides your month is not "did we call everyone?" It's "did we call the right 25 first?"

When the answer is no, the cost is real and large. One missed Grade A lead at a ₹1.2–1.5 Cr ticket is a brokerage hit of several lakhs. Miss two a week and you're looking at ₹ at risk that dwarfs the cost of fixing the process.

## The five scoring inputs that predict a site visit

A property lead's score should answer one question: how likely is this person to physically show up for a site visit in the next few days? Five inputs do most of the predicting.

**1. Budget fit.** Match the stated or inferred budget against your project's GCV band. If your inventory sits at ₹90 lakh–₹1.6 Cr and the enquiry signals a ₹1.2 Cr budget, that's a clean fit and a strong score. A ₹45 lakh budget against the same inventory is a mismatch — be honest about it and score it down, because chasing it wastes the rep's best hours.

**2. Location serviceability.** Is the buyer asking about a location your project actually serves? A Whitefield enquiry for a Whitefield project scores high. Someone wanting Sarjapur when you only have Hennur is a lower-intent fit, no matter how warm the conversation feels.

**3. Configuration match.** A buyer asking specifically about the 3BHK when you have 3BHK inventory is closer to a visit than someone asking "what do you have?" Specificity is intent. A named configuration scores above a vague one.

**4. Portal behaviour.** This is the signal most desks ignore. Repeat visits to the price page, a brochure download, time spent on the EMI calculator, a saved listing — these are buying behaviours, not browsing. A lead who viewed the price page three times this week is warmer than one who filled a form once and never came back.

**5. WhatsApp reply speed.** In the Indian market this is the sharpest near-term signal there is. A buyer who replies inside the hour is in active decision mode. One who leaves your message on a single grey tick for two days has cooled, whatever their budget. Reply speed often outranks form completeness.

These map onto a Grade A–F score. Leadkaun computes it across three dimensions — **Fit** (budget, location, configuration), **Intent** (portal behaviour, reply speed), and **Quality** (data completeness, channel) — in under 500ms per lead. You don't need Leadkaun's exact thresholds to start; you need the discipline of scoring every lead on these five before anyone picks up the phone.

## How the queue re-ranks as new signals land

A static score is half a system. Real estate intent moves through the day. A lead who was Grade C at 9 AM — decent budget, slow to reply — sends "yes, Saturday works for the visit" at 11:40 AM. That single message should reorder your entire desk.

The mechanic is simple: every new signal feeds back into the score, and the queue re-sorts. A WhatsApp reply, a second price-page visit, a confirmed budget, a brochure download — each one nudges the grade and shifts the lead's position. Done right, the lead who just said "Saturday works" jumps from position 30 to position 1, and the next rep who looks at the queue sees them at the top.

This is the part you cannot do by hand at volume. Re-sorting 70 leads by intent every time a signal lands is not realistic for a human juggling calls and site visits. The queue isn't only about who's hottest; it's about who's slipping.

## How reps actually work the queue

The behaviour change is the whole point, and it's smaller than people fear. Reps stop scrolling a chronological list and start at the top of the queue every time.

- **Start of shift:** open the queue, call positions 1, 2, 3 — the three leads most likely to book a visit today.
- **After each call:** log the outcome in three taps (Leadkaun's WhatsApp 3-tap logging means a reply gets logged from the chat itself, no app-switching). The queue re-ranks on that outcome.
- **Mid-shift:** when a new signal pushes a lead to the top, the rep gets it next — not at 4 PM when they finally scroll down.
- **End of shift:** the Missed Opportunity Engine flags any Grade A lead that didn't get touched, so it's first thing tomorrow.

The reason this matters in numbers: Grade A property leads convert when contacted within roughly 47 minutes. A queue exists to make sure the 47-minute lead is the one your rep calls at minute 5 — not the one buried at position 38 while they work through cold form-fills. ## Building yours in four steps

**Step 1 — define your bands.** Write down your project's GCV band, the locations you service, and your live configurations. These are the fixed reference points every Fit score gets measured against. Twenty minutes of work, and it's the foundation.

**Step 2 — capture the right signals.** Make sure portal behaviour and WhatsApp replies actually reach your system. A score is only as good as its inputs; if reply speed never gets logged, your queue is blind to the strongest signal you have.

**Step 3 — set the grade thresholds, then leave them.** Decide what makes a lead Grade A vs C, score every incoming enquiry, and resist the urge to manually promote your "gut feel" favourites. The discipline is the value. Let the signals rank.

**Step 4 — make reps work top-down.** This is a habit, not a feature. The queue only pays off if the first call of the day goes to position 1. Managers should check that calls follow queue order, not personal preference.
