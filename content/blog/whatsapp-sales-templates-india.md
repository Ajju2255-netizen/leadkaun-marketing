Most Indian B2B teams already sell on WhatsApp. The lead enquires on IndiaMART, the rep moves the conversation to WhatsApp within minutes, and the entire deal — price, site visit, negotiation, the final yes — happens in that green chat window. The problem is not the channel. The problem is that every rep writes every message from scratch, slowly, and the good ones leave when the rep who knew the customer quits.

This page fixes that. Below are WhatsApp sales templates built for Indian SMBs — the actual copy-paste lines for the first response, follow-ups, site-visit and demo nudges, quote sharing, renewals, and waking up cold leads. Steal them, change the name and one real detail, and send.

## TL;DR

- The fastest reply wins. Send the first WhatsApp inside the hour — Grade A leads convert within ~47 minutes, and a slow reply hands that ₹ to a competitor.
- A template is a skeleton. Personalise the first line every single time or you get ignored.
- Reply inside WhatsApp's 24-hour service window freely; outside it, you need an opt-in and an approved template.
- Three to four spaced follow-ups, then one honest re-engagement — not seven messages in a week.
- Log every WhatsApp reply so the next person can pick up the thread. A deal that lives only in one rep's phone walks out the door when the rep does.

## First response: get there before the lead cools

A lead filled your form or pinged you on IndiaMART at 2:47 PM. By 3:30 they've messaged three of your competitors too. Whoever replies first, with a name and a specific answer, owns the conversation. This is not about being eager — it's about the ₹ on the table going to the fastest responder.

> Hi {Name}, this is {YourName} from {Company}. Saw your enquiry about {ProductOrService} — thanks for reaching out. Quick question so I send the right details: is this for {UseCaseA} or {UseCaseB}? Happy to share pricing and options on a quick call too, whenever suits you.

For a missed call or a one-line "price?" enquiry, keep it even tighter:

> Hi {Name}, {YourName} from {Company} here — I see you were trying to reach us about {Product}. Ballpark it's {₹PriceBand} depending on {Variable}. Want me to send the full breakdown here, or is a 5-min call easier?

Send these inside the 24-hour window and inside the first hour wherever you can. The note that matters: the question at the end does the work. A message the lead can answer in one tap keeps the thread alive. A wall of features kills it.

## Follow-up: add something, don't just chase

The follow-up that fails is "Hi, any update?" It puts the work on the customer and gives them nothing. Every follow-up should carry a reason to reply — a relevant case, a price clarity, a deadline that's real.

First follow-up, same day or next morning, after no reply to the first message:

> Hi {Name}, following up on the {Product} enquiry. One thing worth knowing — we recently helped a {SimilarBusiness} in {City} with the same requirement; they were comparing on price but ended up choosing us for {RealReason}. Happy to share what that looked like. Shall I send a short note?

Second follow-up, 2–3 days later, with a concrete hook:

> Hi {Name}, quick one — {RealReason: e.g. "the festive-season slot is filling up" / "the current rate holds till month-end"}. Didn't want you to miss it. Are you still looking at {Product} this month, or should I check back later?

Notice the blame never lands on the lead. They didn't ghost you because they're rude — the timing was off, or the need shifted. The second message gives them a clean way to say "later" without disappearing, which is worth more than a guilt-trip that earns a block.

## Site-visit and demo nudges: make saying yes the easy option

For real estate, manufacturing, equipment, anything where seeing it closes it — the goal is to convert chat interest into a slot on the calendar. Vague "let me know when you're free" loses. Two concrete options win.

Site-visit nudge:

> Hi {Name}, for {Project/Product} the fastest way to decide is to actually see it — most people make up their mind in 15 minutes on site. I have {Day1, Time1} or {Day2, Time2} open this week. Which works? I'll block it and send the location pin.

Demo nudge for a software or service buyer:

> Hi {Name}, rather than me sending a long PDF, let me show you {Product} on your actual {use-case/numbers} — takes 20 minutes and you'll know if it fits. {Day1} at {Time1} or {Day2} at {Time2}? I'll send the link.

The two-option close removes the open-ended decision. You're not asking *whether* they'll meet, you're asking *which slot*. When they reply with a day, confirm in one line and drop the pin or link immediately — a confirmed visit that sits unconfirmed for a day is a visit that quietly cancels itself.

## Sharing a quote: anchor it, don't just dump it

A quote sent as a bare PDF with "PFA" is a quote that gets compared on price alone and forgotten. Frame it. Tell them what they're looking at and what to do next.

> Hi {Name}, here's the quote for {Product/Scope} — {₹Amount}, valid till {Date}. Two things to note: (1) this includes {KeyInclusion} which {Competitor} usually charges extra for, and (2) for {timeline reason}, locking it before {Date} holds this rate. Want me to walk you through any line, or shall we go ahead?

For a multi-option quote:

> Hi {Name}, sharing 3 options so you can see the trade-off — {OptionA: ₹X}, {OptionB: ₹Y}, {OptionC: ₹Z}. Most {SimilarBuyers} in your situation pick {Option} because {Reason}. I'd suggest the same for you, but happy to adjust. Which direction feels right?

The expiry date is not a fake-urgency trick — it's the ₹ that's genuinely at risk if the rate or the slot moves. Say it plainly and only when it's true. Recommending a specific option also matters: a buyer staring at three numbers with no guidance stalls, and a stalled quote is lost revenue.

## Renewals and re-engagement: protect the money you already earned

Renewal reminders are the cheapest pipeline you'll ever work — the customer already knows you. Send it early, before the lapse, framed around what they keep, not what they owe.

Renewal reminder:

> Hi {Name}, your {Service/Plan} with us runs till {Date} — wanted to flag it early so there's no gap in {Benefit}. Renewing now also locks this year's rate before the {Month} revision. Shall I send the renewal link, or do you want a quick call to review {usage/results} first?

Re-engagement for a lead that went quiet weeks or months ago:

> Hi {Name}, it's {YourName} from {Company} — we'd spoken a while back about {Product}. No pressure at all; just checking whether {Need} is still on your radar this quarter. If the timing's wrong, tell me and I'll stop the nudges. If it's worth another look, I've got something that fits better than what we discussed then.

The re-engagement message has one job: get a yes or a no, and respect both. The "tell me and I'll stop" line is what keeps you off the block list and keeps the relationship intact for next year. A cold lead that politely closes the door is data; a cold lead you keep spamming is a complaint waiting to happen.
