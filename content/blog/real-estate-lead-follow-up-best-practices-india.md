A buyer fills a form on 99acres at 2:14 PM for a ₹1.4Cr 3BHK in Whitefield, Bengaluru. They're sitting at their laptop, comparing your project against two others in Sarjapur and one in Electronic City. They want to talk to someone today. Your rep is on a site visit and doesn't see the enquiry until 6:30 PM, calls at 7, gets voicemail, tries again the next morning — and reaches a buyer who has already booked a site visit with the Sarjapur project. That deal didn't die because anyone did anything wrong. It died because real estate lead follow-up in India runs on a clock most teams aren't watching.

This is the single most expensive habit in Indian residential sales: treating a property enquiry like an email you'll get to, when it's actually a buyer with a four-hour shopping window. Here's how high-performing teams run follow-up so the ₹40L–₹3Cr enquiries don't leak out the side.

## TL;DR

- Property enquiries now arrive from 6+ sources at once — 99acres, Housing, MagicBricks, your microsite, Facebook lead ads, and WhatsApp — and most teams have no single queue to see them in.
- The high-intent window on a residential enquiry is often under an hour; Grade A leads convert when contacted within ~47 minutes, yet most Indian teams follow up after 24 hours and lose the site visit.
- The fix isn't "call faster on everything" — it's a **priority queue** that puts site-visit-likely buyers (right budget, right micro-market, asked about availability) at the top.
- WhatsApp enquiries are usually your highest-intent channel and your least-logged one. Three-tap logging gets them into the queue instead of buried in a chat thread.
- Stale enquiries carry real money — a missed-opportunity view that shows ₹ at risk this week turns "I'll get to it" into "I'm losing ₹62L if I don't call by 5 PM."

## The enquiry doesn't arrive once — it arrives six times

A decade ago a real estate lead came from one or two places. Today a single serious buyer in Pune or Gurugram touches you through 99acres, Housing, and MagicBricks listings, your project's own microsite, a Facebook or Instagram lead-ad, and — increasingly — a direct WhatsApp message to the number on your hoarding.

Each of those lands in a different inbox. The portal leads sit in the 99acres dashboard and the Housing CRM export. The microsite form goes to a shared sales email. The Facebook leads sit in Ads Manager until someone downloads the CSV. The WhatsApp ping sits on one rep's phone. Nobody on the team can answer the only question that matters at 3 PM: *which of the 47 enquiries that came in today is the ₹2Cr buyer ready for a site visit this weekend?*

So reps default to the worst possible order — they work whatever's in front of them, usually the most recent or the loudest. The patient ₹1.8Cr buyer who enquired this morning waits behind the ₹45L tyre-kicker who called twice. The structure is leaking, and no amount of "follow up faster" fixes a queue nobody can see.

## The 47-minute window is real, and it's brutal

Here's the number that should reorganise your day: a Grade A lead — right budget band, right micro-market, clear intent — converts when contacted within roughly **47 minutes**. Not 47 minutes to close. 47 minutes to get them on the phone while they're still in buying mode, still at the laptop, still comparing.

Miss it and the economics flip hard. A buyer evaluating a ₹1.2Cr flat is not loyal to your project at 2 PM — they're loyal to whoever picks up first. Property portal behaviour bears this out: serious buyers send enquiries to four to six projects in one sitting. You're not competing on the flat. In that first hour you're competing on the callback. The agent who calls in 40 minutes books the Saturday site visit. The agent who calls at 9 AM the next day is now the third call the buyer takes, after they've already toured two other towers.

Most Indian residential teams follow up on portal leads after 24 hours — sometimes longer when the lead came in over a weekend. By then the site visit is booked elsewhere, and the ₹6–9 lakh of brokerage or the developer margin on that unit walked next door. The lead was never bad. The clock just ran out before anyone looked.

## Build a priority queue for site-visit-likely buyers

The answer to "everything arrives at once" is not "work harder" — it's to grade every enquiry the moment it lands and let a [priority queue](/features/priority-queue) decide who gets the first hour.

Grade on three things, scored in under 500ms as the enquiry hits:

- **Fit** — is the budget band and the micro-market right? A buyer searching ₹80L–₹1.4Cr in the exact corridor you sell is a different animal from someone idly tapping ₹3Cr penthouses with no financing signal.
- **Intent** — did they ask about availability, possession date, EMI, or a site visit? "Is the 3BHK still available?" is a buying signal. "Send brochure" is a maybe.
- **Quality** — is this a real, reachable person with a verified number, or a recycled portal lead that's been called by nine brokers already?

Out comes a Grade A through F. Grade A buyers — say the ₹1.1Cr Sarjapur enquiry that asked about possession — sit at the top of the queue and get called inside the hour. Grade D and E browsers drop to a nurture track. Your best rep spends the golden 2–6 PM window on the four enquiries most likely to turn into a site visit this weekend, not on whoever emailed last. Calling leads in arrival order means spending your sharpest hour on your weakest leads. A queue fixes the order so the math works in your favour.

## Log the WhatsApp enquiry, or it never existed

In Indian real estate, WhatsApp is not a side channel — for a lot of teams it's the highest-intent one. A buyer who types "Is the east-facing 3BHK in Hinjewadi still available for ₹95L?" is closer to a booking than someone who downloaded a PDF. They've named the unit, the direction, the location, and the budget. That's a Grade A hiding in a chat thread.

And it dies there. The rep is at a site, reads it on the move, means to log it, and three hours later it's pushed up by 30 other messages. It never enters the CRM, never enters the queue, never gets a structured follow-up. The single most valuable enquiry of the day evaporates because logging it meant opening a laptop and filling six fields.

[WhatsApp tracking](/features/whatsapp-tracking) closes that gap by letting the rep log the enquiry in three taps — capture the buyer, the project, the intent — straight from the phone, while standing at the site. Now that ₹95L Hinjewadi buyer is in the same priority queue as the portal leads, graded and ranked, instead of lost in a thread. The follow-up channel and the buyer's preferred channel become the same thing, which is exactly how it should work in a market where people transact crore-plus deals over WhatsApp voice notes.
